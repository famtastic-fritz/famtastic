What this mockup shows

1Visible navigation, not a help link
When the user asks "show me where", Shay-Shay actually navigates the UI to the right page, in front of the user. The page didn't just open — they saw it open. Same pattern Cowork uses to operate this very screen. Trust comes from watching her do it.
2Spotlit target with step number
The Domain field is highlighted with a glow + step number "2". Everything else dims. The eye knows exactly where to look. The callout to the right reinforces with "this is where you change it" and offers two next moves.
3Shay's compact routine form
During a routine she lives bottom-right in this compact panel. Step counter on top (2 of 4 with progress dots). What she just did in the middle. Three controls at the bottom. The original question is footnoted so the user remembers why we're here.
4Three controls, three intents
Continue — Shay does the next step (e.g., she fills the value). I'll take it from here — clean handoff to the user, routine ends, page stays as-is. Stop — bail out, undo the spotlight. No "back" needed; the user is in the app, not a modal.
5Status bar names the routine
show_me_how:settings.domain appears in green at the bottom. The user always knows when a routine is active. When it ends, the bar returns to normal status. Routines are explicit, not magic.
6This eliminates "two surfaces" thinking
There's no Workshop here. There's no Site Studio Chat here. Shay just helped the user do the thing — same Shay you'd see anywhere else, just operating the screen. This is the multifunctional utility model. The two-surface thing only matters when work needs the bench. Most routines don't.
